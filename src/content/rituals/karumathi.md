The Tamil sixteen-day post-funeral observance is the regional realisation of dharmaśāstric āśauca prescriptions (Manusmṛti, Garuḍa Purāṇa), with day-one cremation, day-three ash-collection (asthi-sanchayanam), intermediate ritual offerings, and a day-sixteen final feast closing the period. The bereaved are excluded from temple, kuladeivam, and other auspicious functions for the duration; the feast formally ends the exclusion.

What the schedule does well — and probably explains its persistence — is provide structured grief work with a defined end-point. Bereavement is treated as a status the family occupies, not an internal state to be managed in isolation, and the community is given explicit roles (visiting, cooking, hosting the feast). What the schedule does less well is enforce real social isolation at the moment grieving people most need contact, and it inherits a "pollution" framing that has no factual basis. Modern observance has drifted toward keeping the structure (the sixteenth-day feast remains common even in diaspora) while loosening the exclusions. Tiered yellow because the practice's social function is real and well-attested but its dharmaśāstric justifications are not evidence-based.
