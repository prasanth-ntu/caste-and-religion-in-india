The Tamil first-menstruation ceremony has two structurally distinct components. The seclusion phase — historically eleven to sixteen days, today often shortened — separates the girl from the household kitchen, the family deity's shrine, and (in stricter households) school. The function phase at the end is a public celebration: turmeric-water bath, new clothes, gold jewellery, a feast for extended kin, and an unambiguous announcement that the girl has reached marriageable age.

The literature gives mixed verdicts because the practice has genuinely mixed effects. Public recognition of menarche can normalise menstrual conversation within families that would otherwise treat the topic as unspeakable, and the celebration phase is emotionally meaningful for many participants. The seclusion phase, especially in its stricter forms, causes documented school absence, reinforces the pollution framing of menstruation as a lifelong category, and constrains the girl's autonomy at a developmentally sensitive moment. The honest verdict is yellow: the celebration is defensible, the seclusion is not, and the trend in urban Kongu households is toward keeping the function and discarding the isolation.
