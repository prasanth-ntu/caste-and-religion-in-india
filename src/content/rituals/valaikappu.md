name: Valaikappu (வளைகாப்பு, Bangle Ceremony)
category: birth
tradition_says: >-
  In the seventh or ninth month of pregnancy, the expectant mother is adorned with rows of glass, gold, and metal bangles by her natal and marital kin. The bangle sounds are said to soothe and stimulate the foetus; iron bangles in particular are said to strengthen maternal and foetal bones.
evidence_says: >-
  The sound-vibration and iron-bone-density claims are not supported by physiology — skin absorption of iron from bangles is not meaningful at the doses involved, and foetal acoustic stimulation from bangles is well below threshold for any measurable effect. The robust benefit is organised antenatal psychosocial support: kin gather, gift-giving is formalised, the expectant mother is publicly assigned a recognised status, and isolation in late pregnancy is reduced. Antenatal social support is a well-replicated protective factor against perinatal depression.

The ceremony is held in the seventh month of a first pregnancy (some lines hold it in the ninth, or for every pregnancy). Female kin from both sides bring bangles in odd numbers; the expectant mother's arms are loaded from wrist to forearm in alternating glass, metal, and sometimes gold. There is a small feast, blessings, and gifts of clothes and household goods for the new child.

Stripped of the folk-physiology, what is happening here is straightforward: the community organises an antenatal social-support event for a woman entering the most physically vulnerable phase of pregnancy, builds a kin coalition that will be on call for the birth and the first months postpartum, and publicly assigns her the protected status of "carrying our child". This is the kind of intervention that perinatal-mental-health literature consistently shows reduces depression and isolation. The bangle-sound and iron-absorption claims are not how it works; the gathering and the witnessing are how it works.
