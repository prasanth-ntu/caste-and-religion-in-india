Performed between the sixth and twelfth month of infancy, usually on an auspicious day at the family deity's temple or at home with a goldsmith and elder kin present. A gold wire or a fine needle is used; the right ear is pierced first for boys, the left for girls. The Suśruta Saṃhitā treats this as a medical procedure and prescribes the precise lobe point.

The community-internal "acupressure" framing is best read as a post-hoc rationalisation in Ayurvedic terms — Suśruta names organs reachable from the lobe, but modern anatomy does not support the meridian model. What the ritual does demonstrably accomplish is a controlled piercing under best-available local hygiene, performed at a developmental window where the infant remembers little, embedded in a kin gathering that publicly registers the child. That is enough to justify the practice on rational-basis grounds without endorsing the supernatural claims.
