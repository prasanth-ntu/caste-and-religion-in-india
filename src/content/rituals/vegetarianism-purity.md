The framing inherited from dharmaśāstra and consolidated under colonial-era caste ethnography places vegetarianism at the top of a moral hierarchy and meat-eating — particularly beef-eating — at the bottom. The framing is influential, generates real social pressure, and is internalised in many households' self-image. The framing is also empirically inaccurate at the population level.

NFHS-5 microdata, once adjusted for systematic under-reporting of meat consumption (Natrajan and Jacob, 2018), places true Indian vegetarianism at roughly twenty percent of the population, not the forty-percent figure that headline surveys report. Meat consumption is not confined to lower castes and is not even significantly lower among some upper-caste groups. Kongu Vellala practice illustrates the pattern: most households eat mutton and chicken on festival days and at celebratory functions, observe specific abstention days tied to Kuladeivam vows and certain Tamil months (Aadi, Margazhi), and treat vegetarianism as a contextual practice rather than a lifelong identity marker. The respectful but firm framing: the purity ladder is an ideology worth understanding, not a description of what people actually eat.
