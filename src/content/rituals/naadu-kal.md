tradition_says: Memorial stones are erected for warriors who died defending cattle, land, or kin — and for ancestors whose deeds entered clan memory. The stones are venerated annually, particularly in Kongu villages, with offerings of liquor, meat, and prayer. Many such stones are eventually deified and become village or kuladeivam shrines.

The specific attribution of individual stones to named Kongu Vellala ancestors is often community-internal and not always independently corroborated.

Tolkappiyam describes the rite in six stages: selecting the warrior to be commemorated, finding the stone, bathing it, inscribing it, erecting it, and worshipping it. Surviving stones across the Kongu belt — many densely concentrated in Salem and Dharmapuri districts — confirm the practice from the Sangam centuries onward and demonstrate its persistence through Chera, Chola, and Pandya rule into the medieval period.

For Kongu Vellala communities, several kuladeivam shrines and Ayyanar guardian-deity sites are continuous with, or grew out of, hero-stone worship — the village hero became, over generations, the village protective deity. The boundary between commemorated ancestor and worshipped god is porous in the nadukal tradition, and this matters for understanding why the Kongu religious landscape is so densely populated by local village deities rather than the pan-Hindu Brahminical pantheon. Tiered yellow because the broad historical practice is green-tier well-attested, but the specific oral attributions of individual stones to named ancestors are community-internal and only partially corroborated by epigraphy.
