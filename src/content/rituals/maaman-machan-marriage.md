Cross-cousin marriage is the structural complement to kootam exogamy: the same rules that forbid same-clan marriage actively channel preferred pairings into the maternal-uncle's-daughter slot. In the Dravidian kinship grammar, mother's brother (மாமன்) and his children belong to a different lineage than ego, so the union is structurally exogamous within the kootam framework even though it is genetically a first-cousin union.

The social benefits are concrete: families know each other across generations, dowry negotiation is lower-friction, the bride enters a household where her mother's natal kin are already familiar. The modern complication is genetics. Standard literature places the autosomal-recessive disorder risk for first-cousin offspring at roughly twice the general-population baseline. Within a population that has been strictly endogamous for two millennia and that carries founder-event signatures comparable to Ashkenazi Jews, the practical implication is a clear case for pre-marital genetic counselling and carrier screening for community-specific recessive variants — not a wholesale abandonment of the preference. Tiered yellow because the cultural practice is well-attested and the genetic-risk literature is robust, but Kongu-Vellala-specific quantification is not yet published.
