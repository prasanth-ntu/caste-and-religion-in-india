Performed around the sixth month — though many Kongu families wait until the seventh or eighth, around the time the first teeth appear. The infant is bathed, dressed in new clothes, seated on the maternal uncle's lap or in front of the family deity, and offered the first morsel of rice. In some lines the dish is sweet payasam; in others it is plain rice with ghee and a touch of jaggery. The grain is offered to the deity first.

The ritual codes a real biological event. Around six months gestational iron stores are depleted, exclusive breastfeeding stops meeting micronutrient demand, and complementary feeding becomes necessary — this is modern paediatric standard, not folk wisdom catching up. The community version adds a public moment, a witnessing audience, and a kin coordinator (the maaman, who in the Dravidian kinship system is the bride-giver for the next generation and so an emotionally central figure). The ritual is sensibly rational-basis: the developmental window is real, the food choice is appropriate, the social packaging is functional.
